Why Your 'Realistic' Goals Are Killing Your Growth

Strategic Clarity / January 13, 2025 / 7 mins read

The most dangerous goal is the one that feels “realistic.” It anchors your strategy to what you already have instead of what you could build. And it quietly caps your growth before you even start.

I call this the Anchored Imagination problem—and it’s one of three momentum killers I see derailing founders across 170+ countries.

Here’s how it works: When setting goals, we instinctively constrain ourselves to what seems achievable with current resources, current capabilities, current constraints. This feels responsible. It feels mature. It feels like good planning.

It’s actually a trap.

The Anchoring Bias in Goal-Setting

Psychologists call it anchoring bias—our tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information we encounter when making decisions. In goal-setting, the anchor is usually your present reality: your current revenue, your current team size, your current market position.

From that anchor, you project forward. “We did $500K last year, so $750K feels ambitious but achievable.” “We have three developers, so we can probably ship two major features this quarter.”

The math seems sound. But you’ve already limited the possible outcomes to incremental improvements on your current state.

You’re not asking: What would we need to become to hit $5M? What would the team look like if we shipped ten features? Who would we need to be?

The anchor keeps pulling you back to who you are instead of freeing you to imagine who you could become.

How Anchored Imagination Shows Up

You’re probably paying this tax if you recognize these patterns:

Incremental targets. Your goals are percentage improvements on last period. 20% growth. 15% efficiency gain. 10% cost reduction. Nothing wrong with these—except they assume the game stays the same. They optimize within the current frame instead of questioning the frame itself.

“Realistic” as a compliment. When someone calls your plan realistic, you feel validated. But realistic often means “I can already see how to do this.” Which means you’re not reaching into territory that would require you to grow, adapt, or transform.

Competitor benchmarking. You set goals based on what others in your space are doing. If they’re growing 30%, you aim for 35%. But this chains you to their assumptions about what’s possible. You’re not leading—you’re following with a slight edge.

Resource-first planning. You start with “what do we have?” and work forward to “what can we do?” instead of starting with “what do we want to achieve?” and working backward to “what would we need?”

The common thread: your imagination stays anchored to the familiar.

The GEC Breakthrough

Earlier this year, I was tasked with improving the Founder experience at the Global Entrepreneurship Congress. My first instinct was anchored: run a better version of what we’d done before. Tighter logistics. Better speakers. Smoother networking sessions.

That’s a 20% improvement goal. Optimize the existing playbook.

But something felt off. I knew I was thinking too small—I just couldn’t see past my own experience to find the bigger opportunity.

When I started using AI to expand my thinking, a different question emerged: What if we didn’t just facilitate networking—what if we architected it?

The result was a matchmaking system that pre-connects founders and investors based on their specific inputs: industry focus, investment stage, check size, challenges they’re navigating. Instead of hoping the right people bump into each other, the system creates relevance before anyone arrives.

I’d been anchored to “run a good event.” The expanded frame was “build a relationship flywheel.”

Same resources. Same event. Completely different strategic ambition—and completely different potential impact.

The 10x Reframe

The simplest tool for breaking anchored imagination: force yourself to think 10x.

Not 10% better. 10x bigger.

This isn’t about setting unrealistic targets you’ll inevitably miss. It’s about using the 10x frame as a thinking tool to surface assumptions you didn’t know you were making.

The exercise:

Take your current primary goal. Multiply it by 10. Then ask: What would need to be true for this to be possible?

If your goal is $1M revenue, ask: What would need to be true to hit $10M?

You’ll immediately notice your brain generating obstacles: “We’d need way more customers.” “Our product couldn’t handle that scale.” “We don’t have the team.”

Good. Those are your hidden assumptions. Now you can examine them.

  • Do you actually need more customers, or could you serve fewer customers at higher value?
  • What would the product need to become to handle that scale?
  • What kind of team would you need—and is that team buildable?

The 10x frame doesn’t commit you to 10x outcomes. It reveals the mental constraints that were invisibly limiting your 2x thinking.

Using AI to Break the Anchor

One reason I’ve started treating AI as a strategic thinking partner: it doesn’t share my anchors.

AI doesn’t know what I’ve always done. It doesn’t have scar tissue from past failures that make me cautious in specific areas. It doesn’t assume the constraints I’ve internalized are fixed.

When I explain my situation and ask “What am I not considering because of my background and experience?”, I get back angles I genuinely wouldn’t have generated alone.

Prompts for expanding anchored thinking:

For breaking resource constraints: “I’m currently constrained by [resource]. What are approaches that would work around or eliminate this constraint entirely?”

For escaping incremental thinking: “My current goal is [X]. If I were forced to achieve 10X that result, what strategies would I need to consider that I’m probably not seeing?”

For questioning assumptions: “What assumptions am I likely making about [situation] that a founder from a completely different industry or background wouldn’t make?”

For reframing the game: “I’m trying to achieve [outcome] within [current frame]. What would it look like to achieve the same underlying goal through a completely different approach?”

The goal isn’t to let AI set your strategy. It’s to use AI to surface options your anchored imagination would never generate.

From Achievable to Audacious

The shift from anchored to expansive thinking isn’t about abandoning practicality. It’s about changing the sequence.

Anchored approach: Start with resources → Project what’s achievable → Set goals within that frame

Expansive approach: Start with audacious outcomes → Work backward to requirements → Solve for gaps

The second approach often reveals that the gaps are smaller than you assumed—or that they’re solvable in ways you hadn’t considered.

One founder I worked with was anchored to slow, organic growth because “we don’t have marketing budget.” When forced into the 10x frame, she realized the constraint wasn’t budget—it was her assumption that growth required paid acquisition. The breakthrough was a partnership strategy that cost nothing but her time.

The anchor was invisible until she stretched past it.

The Compound Effect

Here’s what happens when you break the anchored imagination pattern:

Your goals start pulling you forward instead of reflecting where you’ve been. You set targets that require you to grow, not just execute. The gap between current and desired state becomes a creative challenge rather than a source of anxiety.

You stop competing with yesterday’s version of yourself and start building toward tomorrow’s.

One of my core principles comes from Frances Hesselbein: “To Serve is To Live.” Serving at scale requires thinking at scale. And thinking at scale means regularly breaking free from the anchors that keep you playing small.

The realistic goal feels safe. The audacious goal creates the pull that makes growth inevitable.

Which one are you setting?
